Shail Jadav: Decoding Nature's Masterpieces to Craft a Better Future

Nature, with its intricate artistic designs and unmatched engineering prowess, has been a ceaseless source of inspiration for me. The universality of nature's laws, which inform a multitude of technological breakthroughs, has always sparked my curiosity. During my studies in biomedical engineering, I delved deep into the nuances of perhaps nature's most profound masterpiece — the human body. Through this, I not only understood the body's complexities but also explored how technology could harmoniously integrate with it to enhance life.

My passion led me to serve as a biomedical engineer at AIMS Hospital in Ahmedabad. Here, I played a pivotal role, offering critical engineering support during surgeries and across various hospital domains. However, even as I reveled in the seamless synchronization of technology with medical needs, I was starkly confronted with its limitations. This was a turning point, propelling me to push the boundaries further.

This quest brought me to IIT Gandhinagar, where I undertook an ambitious research project aimed at early diagnostics and therapeutic interventions for Parkinson's disease. In collaboration with Dr. Vrutang Shah and Professor Harish P.M. at the SysIDEA Lab, I pioneered the development of the PD EYE. This revolutionary diagnostic tool employs the pupillary light reflex as a beacon to detect Parkinson's in its nascent stages. But we didn't stop there. To address the challenges faced by Parkinson's patients battling hand tremors, we innovated a therapeutic device to offer them solace and better control.

My journey with SysIDEA Lab then took a leap into the realm of Ph.D. research. After the successful culmination of the PD EYE project in July 2018, I embarked on creating a game-changing gait trainer for stroke survivors. This innovation promises to arm physiotherapists with advanced rehabilitation techniques, ensuring impactful interventions at more accessible costs. Our subsequent findings unraveled the human ability to adapt effortlessly to varied disturbances. Drawing from this human trait, we are now on the cusp of designing a stabilization controller for robots. These robots, when navigating through diverse force fields, will mirror the adaptive capabilities of human motor learning.

Every step of my journey has been about understanding, respecting, and enhancing the symphony between nature and technology. In decoding the secrets of the human body and blending them with cutting-edge technology, I strive to craft a better, more harmonious future.

Human-Motor-Learning-Inspired Stabilisation Control for Robots Operating in Divergent Force-field

Humans are able to learn to stabilise their interaction with the environment even in the presence of divergent force-field [Burdet et al., 2001]. Such situations occurs often in many day-to-day tasks, including tool use. Developing similar capabilities in the robotic system is still an open and active area of research[Li et al., 2018, Yang et al., 2011]. The robots require additional stabilisation controllers (such as variable-impedance-learning controllers) for precise and accurate movement in a divergent force field to overcome the instability. The variable-impedance-learning-controllers iteratively learns the impedance parameters of the robot to stabilise the robot in the presence of a divergent force field. Robotic applications such as tool manipulation (drilling, cutting), object picking-placing, and human-robot interaction often exhibit movements that resemble movements in divergent force fields, motivating the use of variable-impedance-learning-controllers for these and similar applications.

The learning mechanisms in humans (hereafter referred to as human motor learning) not only is able to learn to stabilise but also display a rich set of features such as savings, retention and generalisation [Krakauer et al., 2019]. Humans demonstrate faster re-learning when the same perturbation is applied again after a period of no perturbations, and this phenomenon is known as savings. Additionally, the error magnitude starts at lower values in the re-perturbation period referred to as retention. On top of that, humans are good at generalisation, and we can quickly learn a similar task to the prior learned task. These features of the learning process, including savings, retention and generalisation have great practical utility even in a robotic system. However, current variable-impedance-learning controllers do not focus on these human motor learning features. An example of a practical application in which these human motor learning features are useful is a pill-picking robot that needs to modulate its impedance for various batches of pills for good performance. It will be helpful if it learns faster and better when the same batch arrives again, incrementing overall performance.

To develop a human-inspired variable impedance learning controller, it is necessary to understand human movements and control methodology. Estimation of the impedance parameters in a human reaching task accelerated in the last four decades and generated a plethora of knowledge. Present literature inspires the strategies for modulation of impedance parameters of variable-impedance-learning controllers.

We aim to develop and validate the performance of a variable-impedance-learning controller with human learning features like savings, retention and generalisation for robots operating in a divergent force field. We also aim to learn from human motor control literature to provide better strategies for the modulation of impedance parameters of variable-impedance-learning controllers.

Single DOF Gait Trainer

Robotic gait trainers used in physical therapy for gait rehabilitation in humans have been an area of increased attention over the last few years.However, these devices tend to be expensive.

We are working on an alternative approach to these multiple-degree-of-freedom mechanisms are the single degree of freedom mechanisms that rely on mechanism kinematics rather than control of multiple degrees of freedom to achieve natural gait kinematics. These devices tend to be significantly cheaper while not compromising on quality of motion achievable.

Device for early diagnosis of Parkinson’s disease

Parkinson’s disease is a disorder of the central nervous system that affects movement, often including tremors.

To date, the diagnosis of PD is done based on a finding of the clinical motor symptoms, through a history of patient and response to Levodopa. Further, motor symptoms appear only when approx 60% of the dopamine neurons in substantia-nigra are lost.

Using control system theory approach,we are currently exploring diagnostic tool for detecting Parkinson's disease at an early stage, before the onset of other symptoms.

We developed an inexpensive and straightforward device for early diagnosis of Parkinson's disease based on the pupillary light reflex.
